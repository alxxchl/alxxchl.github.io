Asakusa

Asakusa is one of the last areas of Tokyo that preserves an Edo-era atmosphere, centered around its historic temple, Senso-ji, and busy shitamachi side streets. Before visiting the temple, be sure to catch the panoramic view from the rooftop of the Kengo Kuma-designed Asakusa Tourist Office and then stop by Azuma Bridge over the Sumida River for the classic photo opportunity of Tokyo Skytree and Philippe Starck's distinctive Asahi Beer Tower. From the bridge, you can also take river cruises, notably to Hamarikyu Garden’s Tea House. To reach Senso-ji, walk through Kaminarimon Gate and stroll along Nakamise Street, a bustling area full of souvenir shops, gorgeous kimono stores, and local snacks. While Nakamise is tempting, ensure you explore Denbo-in Street and Nishi-Sando Street, located to the west of the main path, which includes unique spots like the vintage kimono store Kocho. For culinary delights, make sure to try Age-Manju (deep-fried mochi) at Kokone on Nakamise Street and Taiyaki (fish-shaped waffles) at Kurikoan. For a refreshing break, Yifang offers Taiwanese fresh-fruit and tapioca tea, while green tea enthusiasts should visit Suzukien, north of Senso-ji, which is famous for its seven levels of matcha ice cream, including the reportedly world's strongest No. 7.

Shinjuku

Shinjuku is a popular Tokyo hub known for its intense, neon-lit "Blade Runner" atmosphere, vibrant nightlife, and sprawling area around the world's busiest train station. The best way to experience the vast city view is from the free Tokyo Metropolitan Government Building Observatories on the West Side, where you might even see Mount Fuji on a clear day. The East Side hosts Kabukicho, a district famous for its gritty, brightly lit streets, adult entertainment venues, and host/hostess clubs, watched over by a giant Godzilla statue. While daytime shopping is available in the Shinjuku Sanchome area, the East Side truly comes alive with LED light displays at night. Before heading out, visitors can relax at the beautiful Shinjuku Gyoen National Garden, or visit Sekaido for art supplies. For food, the area offers the undisputedly best vegan fluffy pancakes at Ain Soph., and authentic nighttime culture can be found in the gritty yokocho backstreet alleys; while Golden Gai and Omoide Yokocho are famous, the less-touristy Omoide Nukemichi offers a great experience. Finally, the West Side is home to Yodobashi Camera's Shinjuku West store, an iconic spot known both for being a massive electronics retailer and for its famous neon light displays.

Ginza

Ginza is a sophisticated district known for its spectacular modern architecture, luxury shopping, art galleries, and fine dining, contrasting with a few gritty backstreet alleys. Start at Ginza Six, a multi-level fashion and design complex with an outstanding bookstore, an art gallery, and a free rooftop garden offering 360-degree views, perfect for a chic picnic. The main street is lined with stunning architectural gems and flagship stores, including Ginza Place, Louis Vuitton Ginza, and Mikimoto 2. Also on the main street is Itoya, a famous, multi-story stationery store that includes a vast selection of fine papers. For dining, bills offers stylish lunch, cocktails, and excellent fluffy pancakes with a great view, while casual options include the famous creamy ramen at Ginza Kagari, vegan pancakes at Ain Soph., or light fare at METoA cafe/restaurant. For a Japanese afternoon tea, HIGASHIYA fuses traditional tea ceremony with modern design, pairing tea with seasonal wagashi. Design inspiration can be found at the Ginza Graphic Gallery and Le Forum (a free gallery atop Hermès). Other must-visit retail destinations include the massive 7-floor global flagship store of MUJI Ginza (featuring a diner, market, and hotel), and Dover Street Market Ginza for avant-garde fashion; the latter also has a serene, hidden rooftop garden and shrine. Another great, free viewing spot is the Kiriko Terrace rooftop garden and pool atop Tokyu Plaza. Finally, for an authentic, local food experience with a gritty feel, check out the Sanchoku Inshokugai backstreet alley beneath the JR railway tracks, featuring small eateries each serving food from different Japanese regions.

Roppongi

Roppongi, once known for its shady nightlife, has transformed into Tokyo's major contemporary art hub, hosting institutions like The National Arts Center, Tokyo, Suntory Art Museum, 21\_21 DESIGN SIGHT, and the Mori Art Museum, and hosting events like the Tokyo International Film Festival. The luxury complex Tokyo Midtown is a central destination, housing the **Suntory Museum of Art**, high-end fashion, design, and gourmet boutiques (like the traditional sweets producer Toraya), and design shops like Time & Style. Visitors can dine at terrace-side restaurants overlooking the beautiful Tokyo Midtown Garden, a prime cherry blossom viewing spot that also features the architecturally significant 21\_21 DESIGN SIGHT gallery and hosts seasonal events like outdoor bars and ice-skating. Continuing south, the main street is less interesting, but heading west leads to Roppongi Hills, which features the Mori Art Museum, TOHO Cinemas, the iconic **Maman Spider Sculpture** by Louise Bourgeois, and a range of shops and restaurants for a great night out.

Marunouchi

The adjacent business districts of Marunouchi and Otemachi, despite their focus on finance, offer superb sightseeing, dining, and shopping. Visitors can get great free views of central Tokyo and the Shinkansen trains zooming into Tokyo Station from the rooftop gardens of KITTE and the Shin-Marunouchi Building (where you can take 7th-floor food to the garden). The area is great for strolling: along Marunouchi Naka-dori Avenue for boutiques, and visiting Marunouchi Brick Square and the Mitsubishi Ichigokan Museum. For a contrast between the modern city and greenery, walk through the Outer Gardens of the Imperial Palace to see the moat and the photogenic Seimon Stone Bridge. Architectural enthusiasts should see the world-famous Tokyo International Forum, known for its impressive interior and zig-zag walkways. For a taste of luxury, the Aman and Palace Hotel in Otemachi offer unparalleled design, cocktail lounges, and afternoon tea with stunning views; the Aman even has a sleek ground-floor café surrounded by an urban forest. Finally, the underground passageway on the east side of Tokyo Station is a must-see for fans of Japanese pop culture, featuring Tokyo Character Street (with over 30 shops dedicated to franchises like Pokémon and Ghibli), Tokyo Ramen Street for regional varieties, and the extensive basement food section of the Daimaru department store, perfect for takeaway meals.

Yanaka

Yanaka is a quiet neighborhood offering an old-Tokyo, small-town atmosphere, starting from Nippori Station down the Yūyake Dandan stairs into Yanaka Ginza, a shopping district filled with small shops and artisanal cafés like CIBI and HAGISO. Popular spots include Himitsudo for famous *kakigori* (shaved ice) and the beautiful Yanaka Cemetery, which features the Tennoji Temple and is a celebrated cherry blossom viewing spot. The area is also an art hub, with galleries like SCAI THE BATHHOUSE and the revived cluster of old houses at Ueno Sakuragi Atari, which hosts the Vaner bakery and Yanaka beer hall. Other unique stops include the retro Mikado shop with its famous Himalayan cedar tree, Nennekoya (a quirky cat-themed café), vintage shops, and the historic Kayaba Coffee. Visitors can conclude their day near Nippori Station at Kosho Same No Ha antique bookstore and the atmospheric Sampota Cafe Nombiriya.

Omotesando

Omotesando and Aoyama are Tokyo's centers for sleek design and hip fashion, known for their stunning flagship stores designed by famous architects. Start at the Tokyu Plaza intersection, ascend to the Omohara Forest rooftop garden for city views, and then explore Omotesando Avenue, appreciating buildings like Dior's tower and Omotesando Hills, and visiting Espace Louis Vuitton for free art. The side streets hold hidden gems, including vintage shops, the kawaii-focused 6% Doki Doki, and the Design Festa Gallery. On Cat Street, find Micasadeco & Cafe for famous fluffy ricotta pancakes. Dining options range from a luxury *kaiseki* lunch at Sashya Kanetanka to unique tea creations at Chun Shui Tang and Sakurai Tea Experience. Retail therapy continues at the GYRE complex (featuring HAY and Cibone) and Spiral Market. Art and nature lovers should visit the Nezu Museum for its ancient arts and traditional Japanese garden, and marvel at Kengo Kuma’s SunnyHills building. For a high-end end to the day, TWO ROOMS bar and restaurant offers a terrace with great views over Tokyo.

Ebisu

The relaxed neighborhoods of Daikanyama, Nakameguro, and Ebisu are stylish adjacent areas to Shibuya, known for sleek design, boutiques, art galleries, and high-end dining. Begin at Daikanyama’s T-Site, a complex centered around the exquisite Tsutaya Bookstore; enjoy the unique coffee and cocktails at the bookstore's second-floor Anjin lounge, or opt for sweets at Dolce Tacubo. For a traditional break, visit the historic, early 20th-century residence, Kyu Asakura House, which offers a beautiful garden for a nominal fee. Nearby, LURF GALLERY CAFE is a spot for coffee, vintage Danish furniture, and design goods. Heading toward Shibuya, Log Road offers shops and a brewery in a quiet, green oasis. Nakameguro along the Meguro River is famous for its cherry blossom viewing and hosts the enormous Starbucks Reserve Roastery, a Kengo Kuma-designed four-story complex that serves as a coffeeshop, roastery, and bar. In Ebisu, photo enthusiasts can visit the Tokyo Photographic Art Museum or the bookstore NADiff a/p/a/r/t. Conclude the day with outstanding city views and modern Thai food at Longrain, or experience the gritty, late-night food stalls and grilled delicacies at Ebisu Yokocho. Cocktail aficionados should visit Bar TRENCH, consistently ranked one of Asia's best, known for its meticulously crafted drinks and wonderfully laid-back atmosphere.

Nakano

Just a short distance from Shinjuku, Nakano offers a blend of nostalgia and discovery, characterized by its smoky izakayas, otaku stores, and offbeat cafes. The heart of the area is Nakano Broadway, a multi-story shopping complex north of the station. Downstairs, you find corridors of Asian groceries, used kimono shops, and legendary lunch-only spots like Daimon for udon. The upper floors are a paradise for fans of anime and pop culture, packed with stores specializing in collectibles, like Gaocchi and the extensive Mandarake shops. Tonari no Zingaro sells Takashi Murakami merchandise on the fourth floor, while Fujiya Camera offers an excellent selection of vintage cameras. For a break, the hip cafe LOU is located further north on the bar street. The quieter Yakushi Ai Shopping Street offers great dining, including Minami Indo Dining and the local izakaya New York Nakano. Be sure to catch the stunning light at the photogenic pedestrian crossing near the pink kebab shop and local temple. Nakano truly comes alive after dark, with hundreds of izakayas, bars, and eateries concentrated in the side streets east of the station; local favorites include the modern sake bar Sansai, the fusion spot Tototo, and the standing izakaya PachiPachi, near an iconic hidden square. These backstreets, with their retro lanterns and signs, are highly photogenic. For a quieter end to the night, the south side has a few European-looking restaurants and bars, like the Italian-inspired Parpunte, or visitors can enjoy a late-night gathering at Nakano Central Park.
