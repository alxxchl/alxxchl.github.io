Lujiazui

Lujiazui is the glittering, futuristic financial district of Shanghai, dramatically situated on a peninsula on the east bank of the Huangpu River, directly across from the historic Bund. This area is a striking symbol of China's economic ascent and modern urban ambition, defined by a cluster of supertalls that dominate the city's skyline. It is worth visiting to witness this architectural marvel and its sheer scale. You can ascend to the observation decks of towers like the Oriental Pearl TV Tower, the Shanghai World Financial Center, or the Shanghai Tower (one of the world's tallest buildings) for unparalleled, sweeping panoramic views across the city, providing a direct visual contrast with the historical buildings on the opposite side of the river.

The Bund

The Bund is Shanghai's most iconic historical waterfront, serving as a powerful visual contrast between the city's colonial past and its hyper-modern present. Located on the west bank of the Huangpu River, the Bund is famed for its "architecture museum of the world," a stunning collection of over fifty early 20th-century European-style buildings—including former banks, trading houses, and consulates—that once defined the city's status as a global financial hub. Visitors stroll along the wide promenade to admire these grand structures, such as the Peace Hotel, while facing the breathtaking, futuristic skyline of Lujiazui in Pudong, making it the essential spot for experiencing Shanghai's unique, dual identity.

Nanjing Road

Nanjing Road is Shanghai's most famous and historic shopping street, serving as the city's commercial artery since the mid-19th century and remaining a bustling center of commerce today. The street is primarily divided into Nanjing East Road, a vibrant pedestrianized zone famous for its large department stores, traditional Chinese shops, and constant flurry of activity, and the more upscale Nanjing West Road, which features luxury boutiques and high-end malls. The road is worth visiting to experience its lively atmosphere, historic retail landmarks, and the dazzling spectacle of neon signs after dusk. Visitors can ride the sightseeing tram along the pedestrian street, shop for both local and international brands, and sample classic Shanghai snacks and desserts.

Yu Garden

Yu Garden is a magnificent classical Chinese garden located in the heart of Shanghai's Old City, originally built during the Ming Dynasty (1559 AD). This tranquil oasis is a celebrated example of classical Southern Chinese garden architecture, known for its intricate design, delicate rockeries, tranquil ponds, winding bridges (like the famous Nine-Turn Bridge), and exquisite pavilions. The garden is worth visiting for its historical beauty and to experience a serene retreat from the bustling city. Surrounding the garden is the bustling Yu Garden Bazaar and the City God Temple, where visitors can shop for traditional crafts and taste authentic Shanghai snacks, such as the famous soup dumplings (xiaolongbao).

Huainan Road

Huaihai Road is one of Shanghai's most elegant and storied avenues, renowned for its refined and sophisticated atmosphere, often compared to the Champs-Élysées. It is worth visiting as a premier shopping destination that blends international luxury with unique Shanghai Haipai (Shanghai style) culture. The road features flagship stores for high-end international brands, sophisticated malls like IAPM, and historic architecture left over from the former French Concession era. You can go to shop at designer boutiques, explore innovative retail spaces like TX Huaihai, and enjoy the charming ambiance of the plane tree-lined street, making it a favorite spot for fashion-conscious locals and visitors alike.

Xintiandi

Xintiandi is an affluent, pedestrianized complex that ingeniously repurposes traditional Shanghai shikumen (stone-gate) houses into a modern cultural and commercial hub. It is worth visiting for its unique architectural preservation, where the exterior facades of the 19th-century residential structures are maintained, while the interiors have been entirely transformed into stylish, high-end restaurants, cafes, boutiques, and art galleries. You can go to enjoy sophisticated outdoor dining, people-watch at the upscale cafes, and experience a vibrant mix of history and contemporary urban leisure, making it a definitive showcase of modern Shanghai's chic lifestyle.

Wukang Road

Wukang Road is one of the most celebrated streets within the former French Concession, famed for its quiet elegance and rich architectural history. It is worth visiting because it is a living museum of Shanghai's 20th-century past, lined with beautiful plane trees and dotted with over a dozen historical buildings, including the iconic, wedge-shaped Wukang Mansion. You can go to take a leisurely stroll, admire the diverse styles of European-influenced villas that once housed famous residents, and enjoy the numerous trendy, independent cafes and boutiques that contribute to the street's refined, artistic atmosphere and status as a top spot for street photography.

Tianzifang

Tianzifang is a unique, labyrinthine network of old residential nongtang (alleyways) in the French Concession area that has been successfully converted into one of Shanghai's most popular arts and crafts enclaves. It is worth visiting for its distinctive, charmingly chaotic atmosphere and its status as a vibrant creative hub where traditional architecture meets small, independent businesses. You can go to explore the narrow lanes, browse numerous art studios, designer boutiques, unique handicraft shops, and relax at the dozens of hidden cafes and bars tucked away in the former residential spaces, offering a glimpse into a repurposed piece of "Old Shanghai."

Jingan Temple

Jing'an Temple is a magnificent, active Buddhist monastery with a history spanning over 780 years, providing a striking spiritual sanctuary right in the heart of Shanghai’s bustling central commercial district. It is worth visiting because of the profound visual contrast it offers: its resplendent, golden-roofed halls stand directly adjacent to—and in dramatic opposition with—the towering, glass-and-steel skyscrapers of Nanjing West Road. You can go to admire its ornate architecture, including the grand Mahavira Hall and the largest pure jade Buddha statue in mainland China, and experience a moment of ancient tranquility amidst the modern, high-energy pace of urban Shanghai.
